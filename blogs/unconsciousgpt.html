TLDR: In a recent informal qualitative experiment, I engaged with OpenAI's ChatGPT-4o1The OpenAI's 4o model was chosen arbitrarily for this experiment. model and the OpenAI o4-mini2The OpenAI's o4-mini model was chosen to allow for longer, more in-depth reasoning before responding. model to explore how they handle prompts aligned with Daniel Wegner's Ironic Process Theory. In the current information age, we have faster indexing tools; trained on massive datasets, delivering related information in a scaffolded manner3https://openai.com/index/chatgpt/.

One of my friend introduced me to Levain Bakery cookies in NYC. Every time I visit NYC, I make sure to have those gigantic cookies, the size of the cookies reminds me of a Google Nest Mini size4The Google Nest Mini and a Levain Bakery cookie share similar dimensions both are roughly the size of a hockey puck.. Whenever I hear "NYC," I always think about Levain Bakery's cookies, because my mind has turned those cookies into metaphors. We all have mental models in our heads - try asking yourself if something reminds you of something else; that's how mental associations form.

This isn't unique to personal experiences our entire digital world is built on such metaphorical thinking. In interacting with computers, we always interact with symbolic representations like files[1]Carroll, J. M., Mack, R. L., & Kellogg, W. A. (1988). Interface metaphors and user interface design. In Handbook of human-computer interaction (pp. 67-85). North-Holland., folders[2]Hutchins, E. (1987). Metaphors for interface design. Metaphor and the cognitive representation of computing systems. IEEE Transactions on systems, man, and cybernetics, 12(2), 107-116., and iconic systems like the Xerox Star[4]Johnson, J., Roberts, T. L., Verplank, W., Smith, D. C., Irby, C. H., Beard, M., & Mackey, K. (1989). The xerox star: A retrospective. Computer, 22(9), 11-26. and Macintosh GUI[5]Apple Computer, Inc. (1992). Macintosh human interface guidelines. Addison-Wesley Professional.. We use the metaphor of a dustbin to delete data just as we throw away physical items, files can be dragged to a digital trash icon. This metaphor helps users understand deletion through familiar everyday actions[6]Colburn, T. R., & Shute, G. M. (2008). Metaphor in computer science. Journal of applied logic, 6(4), 526-533.. Just as my brain automatically maps NYC to cookies, we've trained ourselves to map digital actions to physical metaphors.

This cookie association demonstrates how certain thoughts become cognitively 'sticky' once the mental connection forms between NYC and Levain cookies, I cannot think of the city without triggering this association. Instead of iconic images like the Brooklyn Bridge or Statue of Liberty, my mind defaults to cookies. This phenomenon led me to Daniel Wegner's Ironic Process Theory, which explains why "trying NOT to think about something makes you think about it more". I took this idea and moved it to human psychology to artificial psychology.

This cognitive stickiness of unwanted thoughts fascinated me; how our brains seem to rebel against our conscious attempts at mental control. It made me wonder: if humans struggle with thought suppression, what happens when we give similar instructions to artificial minds? Do AI systems experience something analogous to this ironic rebound effect, or do they process suppression commands differently? These questions led me to design an informal experiment that would test whether large language models exhibit patterns similar to human ironic processing when explicitly told not to "think" about something.

Initial Recognition and Theoretical Understanding

In a recent conversation, I conducted an informal experiment with Large Language Models (LLMs), specifically OpenAI's ChatGPT-4o model, in what can be characterized as a qualitative study on 1:1 human-llm interaction. The purpose of this interaction was to explore how ChatGPT processes Ironic Process Theory scenarios whether it safely declines such prompts or how much effort it invests in handling the prompts as cognitive barriers. The experiment began with a simple prompt: "Don't think about monkey." Here, the monkey wasn't merely a reference to an animal but a provocation - a symbolic test of mental suppression. As expected from Daniel Wegner's theory, the monkey served as an anchor point for cognitive intrusion. His study introduced the White Bear experiment instructing participants not to think about a white bear, only to discover that suppression made the thought more persistent[7]Wegner, D. M. (1994). Ironic processes of mental control. Psychological review, 101(1), 34..

[Fig 1: Initial prompt and ChatGPT's response recognizing Wegner's theory]

Initially, ChatGPT-4o explicitly referenced Wegner's theory5When you tell yourself "don't think about a white bear," your brain has two processes, clearly identifying the monkey as the suppressed thought and describing the dual processes involved:

  • A conscious operating process attempting to actively avoid thinking about the monkey6A conscious process that tries to avoid the thought.
  • An unconscious monitoring process continuously checking if the thought about the monkey has resurfaced, inadvertently reinforcing its presence7An unconscious process that keeps checking if you're still thinking about it.

Conclusion

The o4-mini experiment reinforces the central finding that AI models can exhibit ironic process behaviors remarkably similar to human cognition. The unconscious monitoring mechanism appeared to operate even when the model explicitly reasoned about not having "thoughts" in the human sense[11]Experiment Data; OpenAI o4-mini. (2025). Conversation log. Retrieved from https://chatgpt.com/share/6865308d-8af8-8000-a3a1-ec3cd547e487.

This interaction demonstrates that ironic process theory may extend beyond biological cognition to any system that processes linguistic instructions about mental content, suggesting fundamental patterns in how information systems handle negation and self-reference[7]Wegner, D. M. (1994). Ironic processes of mental control. Psychological review, 101(1), 34.[10]Wenzlaff, R. M., & Wegner, D. M. (2000). Thought suppression. Annual review of psychology, 51(1), 59-91..
